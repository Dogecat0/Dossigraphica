# **Geo-Intelligence and Forensic Risk Assessment: Advanced Micro Devices, Inc.**

**Anchor Date:** February 4, 2026 (FY2025 Form 10-K)

## **1\. Geographic Profile Summary**

Advanced Micro Devices, Inc. (AMD) operates a heavily decentralized, capital-light geographic footprint. The company designs its critical intellectual property and maintains executive administration firmly within North America, specifically centered in Santa Clara, California, and Austin, Texas. However, the physical manifestation of these designs relies entirely on a sprawling supply chain heavily concentrated in the Asia-Pacific (APAC) region \[3\]. Over the past fiscal year, AMD has aggressively expanded its R\&D and advanced packaging footprint in jurisdictions like India and Malaysia, capturing emerging market engineering talent, while explicitly divesting internal manufacturing real estate to insulate its gross margins from physical hardware assembly risks.

## **2\. Headquarters & Key Facilities**

The operational mass of the Company is bifurcated between executive/software leadership in the West and high-volume engineering in the East:

* **Global Headquarters (Santa Clara, CA):** Located at the epicenter of Silicon Valley, this facility serves as the strategic nerve center, providing proximity to hyper-scale cloud partners, venture capital, and software development ecosystems \[3\].  
* **Lone Star Campus (Austin, TX):** Functioning as the primary hub for microprocessor design and server architecture, capitalizing on the robust semiconductor talent pool within the Central Texas technology corridor \[4\].  
* **Markham Hub (Ontario, Canada):** The legacy footprint acquired from ATI Technologies remains one of the premier global centers for Radeon GPU engineering and multimedia R\&D \[5\].  
* **GBS by the Sea Facility (Bayan Lepas, Malaysia):** A newly inaugurated 209,000-square-foot facility housing over 1,200 employees, aggressively aligning the Company’s engineering workforce with the global epicenter of advanced packaging technologies \[6\].  
* **Technostar R\&D Campus (Bengaluru, India):** Billed as the Company's largest global design center, this campus anchors a $400 million multi-year investment designed to capture Indian engineering talent and establish the Company as a partner for local sovereign AI deployments \[8\].

## **3\. Revenue Geography Analysis**

For fiscal year 2025, AMD generated $34,639 million in net revenue, marking a 34.3% year-over-year increase \[3\].

* **United States ($11.36B):** The largest revenue block (32.8%), expanding 30.7% YoY. This is fundamentally driven by aggressive domestic capex deployments by major hyper-scalers like Meta and AWS \[3, 18\].  
* **Taiwan & Singapore ($9.47B Combined):** Billed revenue to Taiwan exploded by 57.1% YoY to $5.18B. This concentration does not reflect local retail consumption but perfectly highlights the billing chokepoint of Original Design Manufacturers (ODMs) who physically integrate the high-value AI components into liquid-cooled server racks before global distribution \[3\].  
* **China ($7.75B):** Revenue to China grew 24.4%, a phenomenon largely attributed to aggressive, late-quarter silicon stockpiling by local entities reacting to an impending wave of U.S. export controls \[12\].

To manage massive cost exposures in countries housing its engineering talent (e.g., India, Malaysia, Canada), the Company utilizes a $2.3 billion portfolio of foreign currency forward contracts \[3\].

## **4\. Supply Chain Geographic Risk**

As a strictly fabless operation, the Company's vulnerability to supply chain shock is severe and heavily clustered around the Taiwan Strait:

* **Wafer Fabrication:** AMD is critically dependent on the Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) for sub-5nm and sub-4nm leading-edge silicon. The near-monopoly TSMC holds over CoWoS advanced packaging means any kinetic conflict or blockade involving Taiwan would represent an immediate single point of failure for AMD’s flagship products \[7, 23\].  
* **Advanced Packaging & Memory:** Strategic partnerships with Samsung Electronics in South Korea (HBM3E memory) and Amkor Technology in Malaysia ensure that parts traverse thousands of miles before even reaching final integration \[26\].  
* **Domestic Reshoring:** To mitigate this bottleneck, the Company is leveraging TSMC's Phoenix, Arizona fab construction, Amkor’s Peoria, Arizona packaging facility, and its Sanmina partnership for US-based final server assembly \[16, 24\].

## **5\. Regulatory & Geopolitical Exposure**

The Company's reliance on leading-edge AI technology firmly places it at the crosshairs of superpower decoupling:

* **US Export Controls (Critical):** Following sweeping Commerce Department frameworks aimed at stunting Chinese AI capabilities, AMD’s advanced AI market in China has fundamentally collapsed. Management expects only $100M in localized sales for Q1 2026, dropping to $0 for the remainder of the year \[12, 19\].  
* **Tariffs & Trade War (Moderate):** Expanding Section 301 investigations into electronics and substrates create high inflationary pressures for imported components, which could severely compress hardware margins \[35\].  
* **EU Antitrust (Low/Moderate):** A highly regulated digital competition landscape driven by the European Commission complicates any prospective regional hardware M\&A activity or sovereign data center lock-ins \[37\].

## **6\. Strategic Geographic Outlook**

AMD’s 2026 strategy exhibits stark divergence from the broader tech sector’s contraction. The Company’s $3 billion OpEx surge in Q4 2025 is funding massive expansions in Bengaluru and Penang to dominate the next generation of data center compute architectures. The company is leaning heavily into strategic partnerships, such as the 200MW "Helios" infrastructure build-out with TCS in India \[43\]. Simultaneously, by acquiring ZT Systems' intellectual talent for $3B and immediately spinning off its blue-collar physical manufacturing footprint in New Jersey to Sanmina, AMD ensures its high-margin fabless identity remains pristine while shedding capital-heavy domestic factory risks \[13, 17\].
